Work

This short lesson will help you talk about work and working.  Most of us have to do it!
The lesson comes in three parts and each part will be explained with a simple dialogue:

  1. Getting work
  2. Working
  3. Leaving work

Part 1: Getting work

Task 1: Read this dialogue and write down the words in red.
Can you write what they mean?
Can you translate them into your language?

John: You look very smart.  Going somewhere special?
Anne: Yes and no.  I've got an interview for that job I told you about.
John: The part-time one in the data centre?
Anne: No, not that one.  I'm fed up with casual work.  I want a full-time job with prospects so I applied to the TV company to work on their website and they invited me for an interview.
John: I hope you get the position.  With your CV, I'm sure they'll be impressed.
Anne: What about you?  Are you still unemployed?
John: Me?  No.  I've got a job at the Tourist Information Office but it's only temporary.  I'll be out of work again at the end of the summer and job-hunting again.
Anne: Well, I hope this one will be the start of a new career.  Wish me luck!
John: Good luck.  Remember to smile!

Now, can you say what all the words mean?
Look at this and click on the to compare what you wrote with the answers.

interview
Noun: (/ˈɪn.tə.vjuː/) a meeting where people ask questions to get information.
The interviewer is the person who asks the questions.
The interviewee is the person who answers the questions
part-time
full-time
Adjectives: (/pɑːt.ˈtaɪm/ /fʊl.ˈtaɪm/)
In a full-time job, you go to work for the whole day, every day (usually 5 days a week for 7 or 8 hours every day)
In a part-time job you do not work a full day and may not work every day.  For example only on Mondays and Tuesdays or only in the morning.
casual
temporary
Adjectives: (/ˈkæ.ʒʊəl/ /ˈtem.prə.ri/)
Both mean not fixed or regular.  The work is not permanent.
The adjective casual is more negative.
prospects
Noun (plural): (/ˈprɒ.spekts/)
Opportunities at work.  For example, I want a job with prospects for the future to go higher in the company.
apply
Verb: /(/ə.ˈplaɪ/), regular.
To put your name forward for something (e.g.: I applied for a university course).
To ask for something (e.g.: I applied for a holiday).
The person who applies is called the applicant (/ˈæ.plɪkənt/).
invite for an interview
Phrase: (/ɪn.ˈvaɪt.fər.ən.ˈɪn.tə.vjuː/)
Get someone to come to a meeting to ask them questions.
For example an interview for a place at university, an interview for a job, a radio interview etc.
position
Noun: (/pə.ˈzɪʃ.n̩/)
A place in a company or organisation.
For example: What's his position?  He's the customer manager.
CV
Noun: (/siː.ˈviː/)
Abbreviation (short form) of curriculum vitae (/kə.ˈrɪ.kjʊ.ləm.ˈvaɪ.tiː/)
A document where you list your qualifications, work experience, interests etc.
unemployed
Adjective: (/ˌʌ.nɪmˈplɔɪd/)
Without a job.
The opposite is employed (/ɪmˈplɔɪd/) and both words come from the verb employ (/ɪmˈplo.ɪ/)), meaning give work to, for example: We employed five new people in the company.
be out of work
Phrase: (/bi.ˈaʊt.əv.ˈwɜːk/)
Be without a job.
Informal way to say unemployed.
job hunting
Compound verb: (/ˈdʒɒb.hʌnt.ɪŋ/)
Looking for work.
This can also be a noun, e.g.: I don't enjoy job hunting; nobody does!
career
Noun: (/kə.ˈrɪə/)
A job for which you have training and in which you can advance to go higher up in the organisation.
For example: He had a long career in business before he stopped and trained as a teacher.  Education is his new career.

Part 2: At work

Task 3: Read this second dialogue and write down the words in red.
Can you write what they mean?
Can you translate them into your language?

Mary: Is that your brother?  He looks very serious.
Anne: Yes, it's his personality.  I guess it suits his profession.
Mary: Why, what's he do for a living?
Anne: He's a policeman.  He works in the computer fraud department.  It's an interesting job but a bit stressful.
Mary: So's mine.
Anne: Oh.  What do you do?
Mary: Me?  I'm a surgeon.  I work for the university hospital.
Anne: Oh, I started to train as a doctor but I gave it up.
Mary: Why?
Anne: Too antisocial for me so I changed track and now I teach.
Mary: Isn't that pretty demanding?
Anne: Probably but not as stressful as working in medicine.
Mary: I guess stress is an occupational hazard!

Now, can you say what all the words mean?
Look at this and click on the to compare what you wrote with the answers.

profession
Noun: (/prə.ˈfeʃ.n̩/) a job which needs a high level of training such as teaching, accountancy, the law etc.
Now the word can be used for any kind of job.  The adjective is professional (/prə.ˈfe.ʃnəl/) as in, for example: He's a professional photographer and it means this is his main job and he is paid for it.
do for a living
Phrase: (/duː.fər.ə.ˈlɪv.ɪŋ//) work to earn money.
What you do for a living is the name of your job.
work in
work for
Verb phrases: (/ˈwɜːk.ɪn/ /ˈwɜːk.fɔː/)
The first tells you what sort of work someone does.  For example:
I work in the oil industry
The second tells you where someone works.
I work for an oil company
stressful
Adjective: (/ˈstre.sfl̩/) difficult or giving you worry and problems.
For example: Driving an ambulance is a very stressful job.
The noun is stress (/stres/) and means worry.  For example: This work is giving me too much stress.
What do you do?
Clause: (/ˈwɒt.də.jə.duː/).
This is the simple way in English to ask about someone's job.
You can also ask: What do you do for a living? (see above).
train as
Verb phrase: (/treɪn.æz/)
Be taught a job.  For example:
He trained as a gardener.
We can also have re-train as (/ˌriː.ˈtreɪn.æz/) to mean learn a new job.
give up
Phrasal verb: (/ɡɪv.ʌp/)
To stop doing something.  For example:
I gave up smoking
I gave up university
etc.
antisocial
Adjective: (/ˌæn.ti.ˈsəʊʃ.l̩/) making social life difficult for example, working at night and at the weekends.
I teach
Verb phrase: (/ˈaɪ.tiːtʃ/)
For most jobs, we use the name of the job.  For example, I'm a doctor, I'm a footballer, I'm a computer programmer etc.
For some jobs, we can just use the verb, for example, I teach, I design websites, I drive etc.
demanding
Adjective (from the verb demand) (/dɪ.ˈmɑːnd.ɪŋ/)
Asking for a lot of work or time.  For example: This is a demanding examination.
occupational hazard
Compound noun: (/ˌɒ.kjʊ.ˈpeɪ.ʃnəl.ˈhæ.zəd/)
A danger that comes with a job.

Task 4: Read the dialogue again and then click here to try a short test to see if you can remember all the words.

Part 3: Leaving work

People leave work for lots of reasons.  There are five reasons in this dialogue.

Task 5: Read the dialogue and write down the words or phrases which refer to leaving or stopping work.
Can you see the differences?
Can you translate them into your language?

Fred: Does your wife still work at the hospital?
John: No, she resigned last year.
Fred: She quit?  Why?
John: They wouldn't pay her enough, so she handed in her notice.  Does Peter still work in London?
Fred: No, he was laid off.
John: Oh.  What happened?
Fred: The company was in trouble and made lots of people redundant.
John: Oh, well I guess it's better than being fired or given the sack!
Fred: What about your daughter?  I haven't seen her for a while.
John: She's taken leave for a couple of months to go travelling.
Fred: Jenny's taken leave, too, but it's maternity leave.  The baby's due in March.
John: And you?
Fred: I'm like you; retiring soon.
John: Didn't you know?  I took early retirement last August.

Can you find the eleven expressions that refer to leaving work?

Task 6: Read the dialogue again carefully and write down the words or phrases which refer to leaving or stopping work.
Can you see the differences?
Can you translate them into your language?
Click here when you have an answer.
  1. she resigned
    /rɪ.ˈzaɪnd/
    If you resign a job, you leave because you want to.
  2. she quit
    /kwɪt/
    An informal way to say resigned.
  3. she handed in her notice
    /ˈnəʊ.tɪs/
    This means the same as resign.  You tell the company that you will leave at a certain time in the future.
  4. laid off
    /leɪd.ɒf/
    This means that the company has no more work for you so they can't pay you any longer.
  5. be made redundant
    /rɪ.ˈdʌn.dənt/
    This means that your job has disappeared and you are no longer needed.
  6. be fired
    /ˈfaɪəd/
    Be told to leave your job because you have done something wrong or can't do the work well enough
  7. be given the sack
    The same!
    /ɡɪv.n̩.ðə.sæk/
  8. take leave
    /liːv/
    This means to stop working for a time, usually longer than a holiday.
  9. maternity leave
    /mə.ˈtɜː.nɪ.ti.liːv/
    This is time off work to have a baby and look after it for a while.  Fathers sometimes get paternity leave.
  10. retiring
    /rɪ.ˈtaɪər.ɪŋ/
    Finishing work because you have reached 60 or 65 years of age.  The verb is retire (/rɪ.ˈtaɪə/) and is regular.
  11. take early retirement
    /teɪk.ˈɜː.li.rɪ.ˈtaɪə.mənt/
    Finish working before you reach the official age.
