Delta Module One Course

Syllabus area 5
Knowledge of resources, materials and reference sources for language learning

This section of the syllabus covers these two areas and you should be able to:

  • Critically evaluate a range of traditional materials and e-resources and materials for use by language learners
  • Identify and evaluate appropriate practical uses of traditional materials and e-resources and materials for application in a range of teaching contexts

To be able to do this you need a knowledge of these areas.  The guides on the right are those on this site which cover the areas.  You should follow those and then return to this page to try some tests of your knowledge.

The very brief overview of what you need to know

Before you start, it is useful to consider what the terms Critically evaluate and Identify and evaluate mean.  Click here when you have done that.

Critically evaluate
This is much more than simply saying "This is good", "That is OK", "This is unusable" etc.
Critical evaluation involves considering the targets of materials and exercises as well as understanding the needs of the learners.
It also requires you to identify the underlying premises concerning how the authors understand language and believe language is learned.  For this area, see the references in this course in the sections on theoretical perspectives and approaches and methodologies.
Identify and evaluate
This does not mean the same thing.  Here, you are being asked to focus on the practical use of the materials.
Evaluation again involves understanding what theories of language and language learning underlie the design of the materials and, given the target audience for them, deciding (i.e. evaluating) how useful and effective they are likely to be.

The next steps

If you have not already done so, follow the guides to culture and learning styles and how learning happens (especially the final section on factors affecting learning) and read the articles on culture.
The guides on this site to inferencing and noticing are also relevant.

Now you are well placed to browse the materials on the shelves in your institution or home and evaluate them by asking these questions:

  1. For what kind of learners are they intended?  Consider if they are appropriate and appealing bearing in mind:
    1. Age
    2. Interests
    3. Purposes for learning
    4. Cultures
    5. Personalities
    6. Setting and course type
    7. Learning styles
  2. What theory (if any) of what language is lie behind the design of the materials?  Consider:
    1. Do the designers believe language is primarily a set of skills?
    2. Do the designers believe language is a set of grammatical structures and lexis?
    3. Do the designers believe that language is a means of communication and a set of communicative functions and notions?
  3. What theory (if any) of learning lies behind the design of the materials?  Consider:
    1. Is learning a matter of acquiring good habits?
    2. Is learning an active cognitive process which requires learners to develop and refine hypotheses about the language and its structures?
    3. Is learning enhanced by repetition and imitation?
    4. Is learning a collaborative process?
    5. Can language be learned (or acquired) by solving problems and undertaking tasks?

Once you have done all that plus a little research and worked your way through the guides mentioned above, it's time to test your knowledge in these areas and then do some revision exercises.
