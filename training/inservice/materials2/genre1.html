Genres: reading and writing skills

When texts share the same general purpose in the culture, they will often share the same obligatory and optional structural elements and so they belong to the same genre or text type.
(Butt et al, p9)

There are two things to notice highlighted above.

  1. general purpose: this refers to the reason for a text.  What is it doing?
  2. structural elements: what is each part of the text doing?

The next thing to note is that genre is not defined in its literary, musical or lay sense here.  In those registers genre includes, e.g., science fiction, romance, thriller, detective, heavy metal, folk, classical etc.  That is not a useful definition of genre for our purposes.

General purpose

What are the purposes of texts?  Think for a moment and then click here to see how many purposes you have thought of.

Here's a short list of the most commonly encountered genres:

RECOUNT
the setting out of a sequence of events to say what happened and describe their significance
NARRATIVE
to retell a set of events with a problematic or unexpected outcome to entertain and/or instruct
PROCEDURE
to explain how to do something
INFORMATION REPORT
to provide information about something
EXPLANATION
to say why and how something happens
EXPOSITION
to argue a case
DISCUSSION
to present both sides of a case before (possibly) coming to a decision

Can you think of example for each of the above?  Click here when you have a list.

Here are some ideas:

Genre or text type Examples
RECOUNT an anecdote, an excuse for being late, a funny thing happened to me on the way to ...
NARRATIVE a novel, a short story, fables, parables, jokes
PROCEDURE recipes, manuals, maintenance instructions
INFORMATION REPORT encyclopedia entries giving information about things, text book sections, grammar books, reports of experiments
EXPLANATION web pages like this, encyclopedia entries explaining processes
EXPOSITION political pamphlets, leader columns in papers, letter to the editor
DISCUSSION academic texts, student essays, histories

It is not hard to see that there is potential overlap between some of these.
Recount and Narrative are similar as are Procedure, Information report and Explanation; Exposition and Discussion are often confused.  The similarities and differences can be seen in the table below.

Theme and Rheme

You are probably familiar with the idea that the first sentence of many paragraphs in written texts signals the writer's theme for the paragraph.  This is often called the 'topic sentence'.  Consider this paragraph from a popular novel (La Plante, 2006):

Arriving at Milan airport, Anna passed through customs way behind Langton and Professor Marshe.  They seemed to be in deep conversation; he was constantly bending down to listen to her, guiding her with one hand at the small of her back.  There was a familiarity about them that Anna found upsetting, though she had no right to feel that way.

The first clause sets the scene and is called the Theme.  The Rheme of that is Anna passed through customs, which is followed by the next Theme, Langton and Professor Marshe.  The two characters are referred to with the pronoun they and form the Theme of the next clause which has as its Rheme he was constantly bending down to listen to her, guiding her with one hand at the small of her back.  This in turn is transformed into the Theme of the final sentence, a familiarity about them.  Thus do well written paragraphs hang together and guide the reader smoothly through the text.
