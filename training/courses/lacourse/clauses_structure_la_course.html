Clause structures, verbs, tenses, aspect and modality


they met outside

Clauses are independent units of meaning.  They are, grammatically, phrases which contain at least one verb phrase.  Compare, for example the items here.  The bits on the right are clauses; those on the left are phrases:

Phrases Clauses
in the huge garden he arrived
very, very slowly playing the piano
an old dog to help with the cooking
the woman on the corner she obviously left early
the engineer's wife going slowly under the bridge

All the chunks on the right here contain a verb of some sort but none on the left does.
However, if you have spotted that only he arrived and she left early can stand alone as pieces of intelligible language, you have noticed something rather important.  A definition of a clause used in many traditional grammars is that it is a unit containing a subject and its predicate.

  • The subject is the thing or person that does whatever the verb suggests.
  • The predicate can be many things.
    • In the example above, they met outside, it is the adverb: outside
    • It can be a prepositional phrase: They met outside the pub
    • It can be a noun phrase (as an object): They met me
    • It can be another clause: They met because they needed to talk

Finite and Non-finite clauses

In this analysis, we will be using the term 'clause' for any group of words containing a verb phrase but will distinguish between finite and non-finite clauses.

  • A finite verb, as in the sentences above, is a form that is marked in some way for tense (the past of meet, in this case) or for person (such as adding -s to the verb to make He meets).  In English, many verbs which are finite (i.e., carrying tense and person) do not have an obvious marker in the morphology.  They are still finite forms because having no obvious mark is called a zero marker.
    All of these are clauses with finite verbs (i.e., they are finite clauses):
    • He arrived at 6 (marked for tense with -d on the verb)
    • We start at 7 (no marking for tense or person but zero marked and finite)
    • We are starting at 8 (marked for tense [current arrangement to talk about the future] and person [plural are])
  • A non-finite verb form is not marked for tense or person, even by zero, and we do not, by looking at it, know when it happens or who does it.  All these are non-finite clauses:
    • opening the letter ...
    • ... to see clearly
    • having seen it ...
    • to speak honestly ...

Matrix and Subordinate clauses

In geology, a matrix is a fine-grained rock in which other minerals are embedded and the definition will hold quite well for our purposes.
Consider these two sentences:

  1. She saw the dog wanted food
  2. She saw the dog wanted to eat something
In sentence 1., we have two clauses:
The Matrix clause: She saw the dog wanted food
The Subordinate clause embedded in the matrix: the dog wanted food
Both of these clauses are finite because the verb is marked for tense (and in many languages would also be marked for aspect and person).
In sentence 2., we have three clauses:
The Matrix clause: She saw the dog wanted to eat something
Subordinate clause A: the dog wanted to eat something
Subordinate clause B: to eat something
Both the Matrix clause and Subordinate clause A are finite clauses with the verb marked for tense (saw and wanted respectively).
Subordinate clause A is embedded in the Matrix clause.
Subordinate clause B is embedded in Subordinate clause A and is non-finite (the verb, to eat, is unmarked for person or tense).
Subordinate clause A, therefore, is the Matrix clause for Subordinate B.

This means, if you are following, that the terms Matrix and Subordinate are relative.  A subordinate clause can be the matrix clause for its own subordinate clause.
In many cases (as in these examples) the Matrix clause and the sentence are the same.  That needn't be the case because we can have, e.g.,
Mary came home when she finished work and John left as soon as he saw her.
In which we have two Matrix clauses both with an embedded Subordinate clause (of time) but only one sentence.

There are two important points:

  1. All matrix clauses must be finite clauses
  2. Subordinate clauses can be finite or non-finite

Non-finite clauses contain one of the following verb forms:

  • the bare infinitive: She let me leave early
  • a to-infinitive: I want to leave early
  • a past participle form: Left on the table were the remains of dinner
  • an -ing form: Leaving early was a real bonus.

Finite verb forms will always be marked for tense (even if as in, e.g., They come late, the marking for tense is the absence of a change to the verb or an ending) and often for person, too, as in e.g., He comes late.

There will be more about types of subordinate clauses later in the course.

Verbless clauses

This sounds like a contradiction in terms because we have just defined a clause as a unit containing a verb phrase.  At times, however, we can leave out the verb because it will be easily understood.  We also, incidentally, often have to leave out the verb's subject as well.
Here are some examples:

Leaving out the finite verb phrase
If possible, come before six (= If it is possible, come before six)
Whether now or later, we'll get it done (= Whether we do it now or later, we'll get it done)
These clauses often contain conjunctions such as whether, whenever, where etc.
Leaving out a non-finite verb phrase
Too tired to cook, I went straight to bed (= Being too tired to cook, I went straight to bed)
There are lots of interesting jobs in the sector, many highly paid (= There are lots of interesting jobs in the sector, many being highly paid)

Verbless clauses are sometimes called defective clauses or even simply small clauses.

Tenses and aspects

This is a short course so we will not try to analyse tenses and aspects in any depth.
For much more in this area, go to the initial plus index to verbs and tenses.

You are probably aware that all languages have a way of saying when something happened so we can immediately see the difference between, for example:
    I am speaking French
    I spoke French
    I will speak French
and so on.
You will also be able to see immediately that the first refers to something the present, the second refers to a time in the past and the third refers to the future.
The first is a present tense, the second is a past tense and, although it is not really a tense because the form of the verb doesn't change, the third works like a future tense.

What you may be less sure about is the difference between these pairs of clauses:

  • I speak French
    vs.
    I am speaking French
  • I spoke French
    vs.
    I have spoken French
  • I spoke French
    vs.
    I was speaking French
  • I will speak French
    vs.
    I am going to speak French

The difference lies in something called aspect and there are four in English which cause problems for learners (and others) and you should know about.
Briefly, the four are:

  1. Simple
    The simple aspect refers to single events or things which are true whether we are talking about the present, the past or the future.  All of these are simple aspect forms:
    1. I like ice cream
      This is permanently the case so refers not to the present but to all times.  Confusingly, the form is called the present simple but it isn't really a present tense.
    2. I ate an ice cream
      This refers to a single event set in the past and we could add, for example, last Tuesday, yesterday, when I was in Margate and lots of other fixed-time expressions.
    3. I will eat an ice cream later
      This refers to my prediction about an event in the future.  It will probably come true but it might not.  The future is like that.
  2. Progressive
    As the name implies, this refers to ongoing events.
    These two examples are in the progressive aspect:
    1. I am eating an ice cream
      This describes a current ongoing event.  We could add, for example, at the moment, as I speak, now and other time expressions referring to the present.
    2. I was eating an ice cream
      This describes an event which was ongoing in the past.  We often add something like ... when you called, while I was sitting in the coffee shop and so on to say either that the event was interrupted or what else was happening at the same time.
    In neither of these cases is it made clear when (or if) the action stopped.
  3. Perfect
    This is the most difficult one for many people to understand because it does not exist in lots of languages.
    The perfect aspect relates two times together.  These two examples are in the perfect aspect:
    1. I have just eaten an ice cream
      This puts a past event inside the present and we will often follow it with something like ... so I'm not hungry to make it clear that the past event has changed the present.  The statement is about the present.
    2. I had eaten an ice cream
      This does the same kind of thing but puts a pre-past even inside the past and it could be followed by ... and I felt a bit sick so makes it clear that event one changed event two in some way.
  4. Prospective
    This refers to looking towards the future not just talking about the future.
    Here are two examples:
    1. I am going to buy an ice cream
      This refers to my current intention and it may or may not come true.
    2. We agreed that I am buying the ice creams today
      This refers to an arrangement which exists now concerning the immediate future.  Again, it may or may not come true.

Modality

This course does not have a separate section concerned with clauses which contain what is called modal expressions so this will be the only brief mention.  For more on this very important area, follow the link to Modality below.
Briefly:

Modality refers to how the speaker / writer sees a state or event in terms of four factors:

  1. How likely something is to be true.  For example:
        That will be your mother coming home, I expect
        She can't have left already
        You must be joking
        I guess that's a possibility
        Ostensibly, she's in charge
        She should have arrived by now if she got the right train
    and so on.
  2. How permissible or obligatory something is.  For example:
        You need to get a visa to travel there
        You can go now, if you like
        You aren't allowed to turn right here
        This must be sent out today
        You should see a doctor about those headaches
        Smoking is not permitted
  3. Whether a person is able or willing to do something.  For example:
        Will you help with this?
        Can you see him later?
        Was she able to understand?
        I don't have the strength for this
        Would you like some tea?
        I couldn't wait for her
  4. Whether something is inevitable because of the way the world works.  For example:
        The answer must be more than seven
        A rectangle has to have parallel sides
        Gravity acts equally everywhere
        Light travels faster than sound
        Parallel lines cannot meet
        It is necessary to raise the temperature above 1538° Celsius to melt iron

As you can see from the examples, there are many different ways to signal the speaker's view of these four factors but the most common (and sometimes the only one taught) is by using modal auxiliary verbs such as can, could, may, might, must, ought to, had better, have to, need to, should and so on.
These verbs have special characteristics which are explained on this site.
