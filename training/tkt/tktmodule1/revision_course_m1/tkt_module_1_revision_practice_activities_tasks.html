Practice activities and tasks for language and language skills development

Click here to go to this section of the course.

Make a note of your answer to the question and then click on the to reveal the answer.

Explain the difference between a task and an activity.
Tasks usually have measurable outcomes with the learners getting things right or wrong.  Learners often see them as tests and the product is important.
Activities are more about doing things with the language and it is the process rather than the product which is most important.
What sort of feedback is it appropriate to get from tasks and activities?
If the process is the most important thing (i.e., doing the activity), then feedback can be short and simple.
If the product of the task is important for the teacher to see if the language or skill has been learned, then feedback has to be carefully taken to see if it is safe to go on.
Give one example of each of these:
   controlled practice
   semi-controlled practice
   free practice
Controlled practice:
   a multiple-choice test
   a gap-filling task
   a sentence completion task
Semi-controlled practice
   a role play with instructions
   a find-some-who activity
   an ordering task
Free practice
   a writing task on a topic of the students' choice
   a discussion
   a conversation class
Explain the zone of development and say why it is important.
This refers to the idea that a task or activity must be challenging but not impossible and requires the learner to use language with a little help only.
It is important because if the task or activity is too difficult, the learners will be demotivated and stressed but if it is too easy, the learners will quickly get bored and think they are not learning anything.
